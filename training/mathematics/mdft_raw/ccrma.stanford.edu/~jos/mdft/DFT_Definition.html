DFT Definition

The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) of a signal \(x\) may be defined by

\[X(\omega_k ) \isdef \sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x(t_n)e^{-j\omega_k t_n}, \qquad k=0,1,2,\ldots,N-1,\]

where `\(\isdeftext\)' means ``is defined as'' or ``equals by definition'', and

\begin{eqnarray*}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} f(n) &\isdef& f(0) + f(1) + \dots + f(N-1)\\ x(t_n) &\isdef& \mbox{input signal \emph{amplitude} (real or complex) at time $t_n$\ (sec)} \\ t_n &\isdef& nT = \mbox{$n$th sampling instant (sec), $n$\ an integer $\ge 0$}\\ T &\isdef& \mbox{sampling interval (sec)} \\ X(\omega_k ) &\isdef& \mbox{\emph{spectrum}\index{spectrum|textbf} of $x$\ (complex valued), at frequency $\omega_k $}\\ \omega_k &\isdef& k\Omega = \mbox{$k$th frequency sample (radians per second)} \\ \Omega &\isdef& \frac{2\pi}{NT} = \mbox{radian-frequency sampling interval (rad/sec)} \\ f_s &\isdef& 1/T = \mbox{\emph{sampling rate}\index{sampling rate|textbf} (samples/sec, or Hertz (Hz))}\index{Hertz|textbf}\index{Hz|textbf}\\ N &=& \mbox{number of time samples = no.\ frequency samples (integer).}\end{eqnarray*}

The sampling interval \(T\) is also called the sampling period. For a tutorial on sampling continuous-time signals to obtain non-aliased discrete-time signals, see Appendix D.

When all \(N\) signal samples \(x(t_n)\) are real, we say \(x\in\mathbb{R}^N\). If they may be complex, we write \(x\in\mathbb{C}^N\). Finally, \(n\in\mathbb{Z}\) means \(n\) is any integer.
